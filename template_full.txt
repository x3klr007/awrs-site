SECTION 1: INTRODUCTION
Sustainable groundwater management is a critical component of Saudi ArabiaвАЩs national water
security strategy, particularly in-light of increasing demand, declining renewable water resources,
and the need to preserve scarce and non-renewable aquifers. Effective monitoring of groundwater
wells is essential for ensuring the long-term viability of these resources and preventing
contamination or structural casing failures that could compromise both water quality and well
integrity.
Current monitoring practices, however, remain largely fragmented and inefficient. Although
individual technologies exist for limited tasksвАФsuch as water sampling, downhole wellbore imaging,
corrosion detection, and water level measurementвАФthese tools typically function as isolated,
single-purpose devices. Consequently, achieving a comprehensive assessment of overall well
integrity health requires multiple, sequential deployments using different equipment, often
operated by specialized personnel. This method significantly increases operational complexity, time,
and cost, while also limiting the frequency and spatial resolution of monitoring campaigns.
Traditional groundwater monitoring methodologies rely heavily on manual techniques such as the
deployment of bailer-type sampling devices attached to calibrated measuring tapes. While
cost-effective and straightforward, this approach offers only limited insight into water quality and
provides no information regarding the physical and integrity conditions of the wellbore. As a result,
critical issues such as corrosion, casing failure, or the leakage of contaminants into or from the
wellbore may remain undetected until significant damage or contamination has occurred.
Furthermore, the reliance on single-point water sampling fails to capture the vertical heterogeneity
of aquifer chemistry, potentially leading to incomplete or misleading assessments of groundwater
quality. Parameters such as pH, temperature, electrical conductivity (EC), total dissolved solids (TDS),
and the presence of inorganic or organic pollutants may vary significantly with depth. Without
multi-depth scale sampling and real-time analysis, such variations are often overlooked.
In addition to water quality concerns, wellbore integrity is a critical yet often overlooked aspect of
groundwater well management. Wellbore casing failures, such as microfractures, indepth corrosion,
or poor cement bonding, can lead to the uncontrolled leakage of contaminants or toxic gases into
fresh groundwater zones or the leakage of hydrocarbon fluids from deeper or shallower geological
formations. The absence of integrated tools capable of simultaneously assessing both groundwater
quality and wellbore structural integrity triggers proactive maintenance and risk mitigation
strategies.

Emerging solutions emphasize the importance of transitioning toward integrated, multi-function
monitoring platforms that can perform remotely and real-time diagnostics of both groundwater
quality and wellbore integrity within a single deployment. Such systems can enable continuous
monitoring, allowing for early detection of anomalies and data-driven decision-making for
preventive maintenance and contamination control.
This research addresses the urgent need for a unified, automated monitoring system that
overcomes the limitations of traditional methodologies. By integrating advanced sensing
technologies, including high-definition imaging, X-ray fluorescence (XRF), X-ray diffraction (XRD),
electrochemical sensors, gas sensors and autonomous sampling mechanisms, the proposed robotic
system, AWRS, aims to deliver remote high-resolution, real-time diagnostics of both groundwater
quality and wellbore structural integrity. This technological advancement has the potential to
significantly enhance the efficiency, accuracy, and sustainability of groundwater monitoring
practices, particularly within the context of Saudi ArabiaвАЩs vision and strategic water management
objectives

SECTION 2: BACKGROUND & LITERATURE REVIEW
The foundational principles of groundwater hydrology and well engineering are well-established in
the academic and technical literature. Heath (1983) and Alley et al. (1999) established key
parameters governing aquifer health, including hydraulic conductivity, permeability, porosity,
storage capacity, and geochemical fingerprints such as major ion concentrations (Ca¬≤вБЇ, Mg¬≤вБЇ, NaвБЇ, ClвБї,
SOвВД¬≤вБї) and trace contaminants (e.g., nitrate, arsenic, fluoride). These parameters are critical for
assessing aquifer sustainability, recharge dynamics, and contamination risks. Fileccia (2023) further
codifies structural engineering standards for wellbore integrity, emphasizing mechanical stability
(e.g., casing collapse resistance, cement bond integrity), corrosion thresholds (e.g., sulfide stress
cracking, chemical-induced corrosion), and environmental compliance (e.g., API Standard for tubular
design). However, these frameworks traditionally assume a fragmented operational workflow, where
simple limiting tools, such as bailers for groundwater sampling, calipers for casing inspection, or
resistivity probes for fluid logging and analysis, are deployed separately and sequentially, often
requiring multiple field campaigns (Figures 1вАУ2). This approach not only increases operational costs
but also introduces temporal and spatial discontinuities in data acquisition, limiting the ability to
predict or analyze alarming dangerous issues(e.g., contamination leakage caused by casing
degradation).

Groundwater resources are usually accessed via drilled water wells, which are systematically
registered in national hydrogeological databases (e.g., Saudi ArabiaвАЩs National Water Well Registry).
These records include stratigraphic logs (lithology, permeability), well completion data (screen
intervals, casing parameters), and hydrodynamic parameters (specific capacity). Despite their value,
these datasets often lack real-time monitoring systems, creating gaps in proactive analysis. For
instance, traditional water level monitoring (Figure 3) relies on manual graded tape measurements
or low-resolution pressure transducers, which fail to capture rapid fluctuations in water levels or
contamination events. Similarly, water quality assessments depend on discrete sampling (e.g.,

bailer) followed by laboratory analysis, where samples are sent far away losing their chemistry doing
so. Moreover, this method is prone to sample alteration (e.g., oxidation of Fe¬≤вБЇ, degassing of HвВВS)
and lacks vertical resolution, missing critical zones in contaminants like leaked oil or chemicals or
heavy metals (e.g., Cr, or radioactive minerals).
The global proliferation of groundwater observation wells (exceeding 7 million wells worldwide;
Figure 4) have certain limitations when it comes to the scale of monitoring challenges. Current
practices require field crews to deploy multiple toolsвАФe.g., Meter for water levels, a multiparameter
for in situ water quality, and a hydrocamera for casing inspectionвАФacross separate visits. This
fragmented workflow (Figure 3) not only increases operational costs but also reduces monitoring
frequency, delaying detection of emerging threats such as toxic leakage, chemical contamination or
hydraulic fracturing/cracking of wellbore casing.

Diffuse pollution sourcesвАФsuch as agricultural runoff (nitrates, pesticides), industrial effluents
(heavy metals, solvents), and hydrocarbon leakage (oil and gas)вАФpose systemic risks to groundwater
quality. These contaminants often infiltrate aquifers throughout multiple mediums (e.g., fractures,
poorly sealed wellbore casing), which are challenging to detect using conventional methods. For
example, delayed detection of nitrate contaminations in potable aquifers may necessitate costly
remediation or well abandonment. Similarly, gas migration (e.g., CHвВД, COвВВ) from hydrocarbon
operations can compromise well integrity and groundwater safety, yet current diagnostics (e.g.,
acoustic cement bond logs) lack real-time resolution.
This research gap highlights the urgent need for an integrated robotic system capable of
simultaneous, multiscale diagnostics. Emerging technologies such as X-ray fluorescence (XRF) for
in-situ elemental analysis of casing materials and X-ray diffraction (XRD) for identifying corrosion
products (e.g., FeSвВВ) are well-established in laboratory settings but remain unproven in
field-deployable platforms. Machine learning (ML) models trained on convolutional neural networks
(CNNs) have shown promise in crack detection in civil infrastructure, yet their adaptation to

downhole environmentsвАФwhere turbidity and scaling complicate image interpretation, having it will
improve proactive and remedial actions to mitigate any further damages to wellbore casing or
groundwater quality.

The proposed Aqua Well Robot Solution (AWRS) addresses these limitations by unifying advanced
diagnostics into a single deployment. Key innovations include:
Multi-Sensor System: Integration of XRF (elemental mapping of casing corrosion), XRD
(mineralogical identification of scale deposits), and hyperspectral imaging (organic contaminant
detection) for simultaneous structural and chemical analysis. In addition, gas and oil sensors allow
detection of hydrocarbon leakages into water or out of cracks in wellbore casing
Autonomous Sampling: A hydraulically actuated, hermetically sealed sampling module capable of
retrieving discrete water samples at multiple depths (surface and deeper) without
cross-contamination, preserving redox-sensitive analytes (e.g., Fe¬≤вБЇ, HвВВS).
Real-Time Telemetry: Embedded IoT modules for live data transmission (4G/LTE or satellite) to
cloud-based platforms, enabling AI-driven anomaly detection (e.g., sudden pH shifts, casing crack
propagation).
Adaptive Navigation: SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping) algorithms for autonomous
path planning in complex well geometries, combined with ultrasonic thickness gauges to map casing
wall degradation.

SECTION 3: NOVELTY
The novelty of this research lies in the integration of multiscale sensors and advanced tools into a
unified automated robotic system, enabling real-time in situ analysis and diagnostics of aquifer
waters and associated wellbores. Unlike conventional methods, which rely on discrete, sequential
diagnostic procedures using separate tools and sensors, the proposed system performs
simultaneous, multifunctional assessments, significantly enhancing operational efficiency and data
fidelity.
To the best of our knowledge, this work presents the first comprehensive solution that integrates
wellbore integrity diagnostics with concurrent water quality monitoring in a single deployment. The
Aqua Well Robot Solution (AWRS) is equipped with several sensors, including X-ray fluorescence
(XRF), gas sensors, and high-resolution video capture, enabling real-time assessment of well casing
integrity. The robotic platform is designed for autonomous navigation within water wells, allowing it
to localize leaks, detect corrosion or mechanical damage, and evaluate structural degradation along
the wellbore.
In parallel, the AWRS system performs multi-depth water quality analysis by collecting and sealing
discrete water samples from various depths within the aquifer. These samples are analyzed in real
time using integrated electrochemical and optical sensors, enabling the detection of contaminants,
salinity variations, and other critical water quality parameters. This multisensor approach provides
high-resolution spatial and compositional data across the entire water column.
As a result, the AWRS system eliminates the limitations of traditional, fragmented monitoring
practices, where data are collected separately and later aggregated by delivering synchronized,
high-resolution diagnostics across multiple domains. This integrated and automated methodology
represents a significant advancement in aquifer and wellbore monitoring, offering enhanced
accuracy, reduced operational time, and improved decision-making capabilities for groundwater
management.

SECTION 4: OBJECTIVES
The objective of this research is to develop an autonomous robotic system equipped with integrated
multi-sensor technologies for comprehensive monitoring of wellbore integrity and groundwater
quality. The proposed Aqua Well Robot Solution (AWRS) is designed to perform real-time,
high-resolution diagnostics of subsurface wellbore conditions and aquifer water characteristics with
minimal human intervention.
The robot integrates multiple high capacity sensors, including X-ray fluorescence (XRF), X-ray
diffraction (XRD), and a high resolution camera system enhanced with machine learning algorithms.
These sensors enable the system to assess wellbore casing integrity by detecting anomalies such as
cracks, corrosion, and toxic leakages. In addition, the system includes a high-resolution and real-time
camera to visualize internal wellbore casing under operational conditions.
Moreover, AWRS is also equipped with a remotely operable water sampling mechanism, allowing for
the collection of discrete, hermetically sealed water samples from multiple depths at water surface.
These samples are analyzed in-situ using embedded sensors for pH, electrical conductivity, and total
dissolved solids (TDS), ensuring preservation of chemical and mineralogical integrity during retrieval
and transport. Furthermore, the system includes a depth-sensing module to determine the distance
from the wellhead to the groundwater surface, enabling continuous monitoring of water levels. This
allows experts to predict seasonal droughts and take necessary precautions to accommodate for
potential water loss. The sensors can also detect toxic gas, oil or chemical leakages into groundwater
systems.
All acquired data are transmitted in real-time to a central monitoring station, allowing operators to
make timely, data-driven decisions regarding well maintenance, groundwater management, and
contamination mitigation. This integrated robotic platform represents a significant advancement in
subsurface monitoring technology, offering enhanced accuracy, efficiency, and safety in well and
aquifer surveillance.

SECTION 5: METHODOLOGY

The objective of this study was to design, fabricate, and validate a laboratory-scale prototype of the
Aqua Well Robot Solution (AWRS) for integrated groundwater quality and wellbore monitoring. The
system integrates a multisensor suite to enable simultaneous assessment of wellbore integrity and
groundwater quality under simulated downhole conditions. The robotic platform was developed
with modular adaptability to support future field deployment in operational water wells.
The AWRS prototype was constructed using a scaled metallic chassis compatible with standard
water wellbore dimensions (typically ranging from 4 to 12 inches in diameter). This scale modeling
allowed for functional testing within controlled laboratory environments while maintaining
geometric fidelity for real-world deployment. The robotic system was mounted on a guided steel
cable system, which was integrated with a motorized hydraulic lifting mechanism for automated
vertical deployment and retrieval within the well column.

Key components of the AWRS include:

1)вАЛ Sensor Integration and Data Acquisition
A multisensor array was embedded within the robot body to collect real-time measurements of
critical groundwater and wellbore parameters:
Water Quality Sensors: pH, electrical conductivity (EC), total dissolved solids (TDS), total suspended
solids (TSS), and water level meter.
Wellbore Diagnostic Sensors: X-ray fluorescence (XRF) for elemental analysis of casing materials
(XRD), gas sensors for volatile compound detection, and a high-resolution camera system with
machine learning-based crack detection algorithms.
Sensor data was logged using an built system interface and stored locally for post-processing and
calibration. Data quality and reproducibility were evaluated through repeated trials under controlled
conditions to reach high accuracy and data reproducibility.
2)вАЛ Autonomous Navigation and Control

The robot was programmed to autonomously descend the wellbore and pause at predefined depth
intervals, including at the water table interface, to enhance detection of surface-accumulated
contaminants or leaks.
A control system was implemented to regulate descent/ascent speed and ensure precise depth
positioning for accurate sensor readings and sampling.
3)вАЛ Water Sampling Mechanism
A custom-designed, hydraulically actuated water sampling module was integrated into the robot.
The system allows for the collection of discrete, representative sealed water samples at multiple
depths without cross-contamination.
Retrieved samples were stored in internal chambers for safe transport and subsequent laboratory
analysis, preserving critical chemical and isotopic properties.
4)вАЛ Telemetry and Remote Monitoring
A wireless telemetry module enabled real-time data transmission from the robot to a remote
operator interface. The system utilized a secure, low-latency communication protocol to ensure
reliable data transfer even in deep well environments.
Live sensor feeds and video imagery were accessible via a graphical user interface (GUI), supporting
remote decision-making and rapid response to anomalies such as casing cracks or leakage or water
quality deterioration.
Functional Validation and Performance Metrics
The AWRS prototype was tested in a simulated wellbore environment to evaluate mechanical
robustness, sensor accuracy, and system functionality. On the simulator, the robot successfully
navigated vertical well paths, paused at designated depth markers, and collected representative
data and water samples. Sensor readings were consistent across multiple trials, demonstrating
system reliability and repeatability.
Significantly, the integration of real-time diagnostics and autonomous sampling enables AWRS to
overcome limitations inherent in traditional groundwater monitoring approaches. By consolidating
multiple functionalities, well integrity assessment, water quality analysis, and multidepths sampling,
into a single deployment, AWRS enhances operational efficiency and data resolution.

SECTION 6: PRELIMINARY RESULTS
Lab-scale prototype- proof of concept results:

A laboratory-scale prototype of the Aqua Well Robot Solution (AWRS) was designed and constructed
using a scaled metallic chassis fabricated from corrosion-resistant stainless steel, selected for its
mechanical durability and chemical stability under high-humidity and high humidity exposure
conditions. The chassis was sealed using enclosures to protect internal electronics and sensor
modules from moisture ingress and to ensure long-term operational integrity in downhole
environments.
To further enhance environmental resilience, the exterior surfaces were coated with an electroless
nickel layer to resist oxidation, microbial-induced corrosion, and chemical degradation in
groundwater settings containing sulfides (HвВВS) and carbon dioxide (COвВВ).
An array of multisensor modules was integrated into the robotic platform to enable concurrent
monitoring of key water geochemical and wellbore casing structural parameters. These included:
a)вАЛ Electrochemical sensors for dissolved oxygen (OвВВ), hydrogen sulfide (HвВВS), and carbon dioxide
(COвВВ) detection,
b)вАЛ Optical and potentiometric sensors for pH and temperature,
c)вАЛ Conductivity and resistivity sensors for salinity and total dissolved solids (TDS) estimation,
d)вАЛ Pressure transducers for depth and water level measurement,
e)вАЛ Humidity sensors to monitor internal and external environmental conditions.

Sensor data were acquired via a microcontroller-based embedded system and transmitted to a
custom-developed Interface. This interface enabled real-time visualization, logging, and preliminary
calibration of sensor outputs. The interface system also featured error-checking algorithms and
signal filtering routines to improve data fidelity under variable environmental conditions.
Laboratory Testing and Environmental Simulation
To simulate downhole conditions, a controlled environmental chamber was configured to replicate
the thermal and humidity profiles typically encountered in groundwater wells (temperature range:
20вАУ60 ¬∞C; relative humidity: 70вАУ100%). The AWRS prototype was subjected to multiple test cycles

under these conditions to evaluate sensor performance, chassis integrity, and long-term reliability.
Additionally, synthetic groundwater samples prepared with known ionic compositions, heated, as
well as commercial bottled water, were introduced into the test chamber to validate the system's
ability to accurately measure in-situ water quality characteristics.
Sensor readings were benchmarked against multiple laboratory-grade reference instruments,
demonstrating strong correlation coefficients (R¬≤ > 0.95) across all tested parameters. Notably, the
system exhibited consistent performance over extended exposure periods, with minimal signal drift
or sensor degradation. The mechanical structure remained intact, and no signs of corrosion or
material fatigue were observed, confirming the suitability of the selected materials and design for
real-world deployment.
These results validate the AWRS as a robust and reliable platform capable of withstanding the
physical and chemical challenges associated with groundwater well environments. The successful
laboratory-scale testing provides a solid foundation for advancing to pilot-scale field trials, where
the system will be deployed in operational water wells to further assess its performance under
dynamic hydrogeological conditions.

Summary of findings:
The preliminary results demonstrate the AWRS system is ready for field pilot scale. The AWRS
prototype underwent rigorous multi-cycle testing under controlled environmental conditions to
evaluate its functional performance, mechanical durability, and sensor reliability. To simulate real
downhole environments, the system was exposed to elevated temperature (20вАУ40 ¬∞C) and humidity
(70вАУ100%) levels, while synthetic groundwater solutions with defined ionic compositions (scaled
TDS levels) and were introduced into the test chamber.
Sensor outputs were cross-validated against calibrated laboratory-grade instrumentation, including a
pH and humidity meter. Statistical analysis revealed strong linear correlations (R¬≤ > 0.95) across all
monitored parameters, pH, electrical conductivity (EC), total dissolved solids (TDS), dissolved oxygen
(DO), hydrogen sulfide (HвВВS), carbon dioxide (COвВВ), temperature, and pressure, demonstrating the
system's accuracy and repeatability. Long-term exposure tests further confirmed minimal sensor
drift and no significant degradation in signal integrity over extended operational periods.
Mechanical integrity assessments showed no evidence of corrosion, material fatigue, or seal failure.
The enclosures maintained full water-proof integrity throughout testing, preserving the functionality
of onboard electronics and microcontroller systems.
These findings collectively demonstrate that the Aqua Well Robot Solution (AWRS) is a durable,
high-fidelity platform capable of performing integrated wellbore diagnostics and groundwater
quality assessments under harsh environmental conditions. The successful validation at the
laboratory scale provides a robust technical foundation for progression to pilot-scale field trials.
Future deployment in operational water wells will further evaluate the systemвАЩs performance in
dynamic unpredictable water well settings, paving the way for its adoption as a next-generation tool
in sustainable groundwater monitoring and well integrity management.
